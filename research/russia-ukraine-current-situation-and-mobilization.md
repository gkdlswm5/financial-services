## Headline answers

**The situation right now:** a **strategic stalemate with tactical churn, and momentum has tilted away from Russia.** Russia's 2026 spring–summer offensive has largely failed — ISW assesses Moscow netted a small **territorial loss** over Dec 2025–May 2026. Its one live "main effort," grinding into **Kostiantynivka** at the edge of the Donbas Fortress Belt, is making slow, costly tactical gains that ISW expects to fall short of an operational breakthrough this year. The most consequential dynamic is **Ukraine's deep-strike campaign**, which has forced fuel rationing in Moscow and Russia's first official admission of shortages. Diplomatically it's bleak: the US-pushed **June 2026 deadline lapsed** with no ceasefire and no Putin–Zelensky meeting.

**Will Russia mobilize?** A *declared* second general mobilization remains a **last resort, not the base case** — Russia has deliberately built an entire apparatus (year-round conscription, digital summonses, reserve call-ups, enterprise quotas, record bonuses) **precisely to scale manpower without saying the word "mobilization."** But for the first time, Q1 2026 contract recruitment **fell ~20% YoY despite record bonuses** and dipped to or below the replacement rate — so the probability is **low-to-moderate and rising.** The most likely path is *intensified covert ("crypto") mobilization.*

**Would mobilization change the tide?** Most likely **no — not decisively.** The expert weight (ISW, CSIS, IISS, RUSI, Kofman) reads a new mobilization as a **sustaining/marginal reinforcement and a signal of strain, not a game-changer** — because Russia can't easily train, equip, or maneuver a fresh mass on a drone-saturated battlefield. Its biggest effects would be felt **slowly, through cumulative attrition**, and on Russia's own economy. The genuine minority/Ukrainian view sees real danger if a large call-up lets Russia outlast Ukraine's thinner manpower.

## Part A — The Current Situation (as of ~June 24, 2026)

### Front lines: Russia's offensive has stalled
- **Russia's spring–summer 2026 offensive has largely stalled.** ISW: Ukrainian forces "largely halted" it; Dec 2025–May 2026 Russia gained ~40.6 km² but **lost ~281 km²** of previously-held ground, taking only ~8% as much territory as in the same 2025 period. **[VERIFIED — ISW]**
- **Kostiantynivka is Russia's main effort and the heaviest fighting now.** By mid-June Russia had "likely consolidated some positions" in/around the southeastern city, with Ukraine's defense "deteriorating"; ~100–250 Russian troops reported in the city center. **ISW still judges an operational breakthrough against the Donbas "Fortress Belt" (Kostiantynivka–Druzhkivka–Kramatorsk–Sloviansk) unlikely in 2026.** **[VERIFIED — ISW]**
- **Pokrovsk and Myrnohrad have already fallen** (Jan–Feb 2026) and are not currently contested — but the Kremlin **failed to exploit** the capture into a broader advance; Ukraine now mounts local pushback north of Pokrovsk (near Rodynske). **[VERIFIED]**
- **Other sectors:** Huliaipole (Zaporizhzhia) active but **static**; Ukraine **advanced** near Orikhiv/Lobkove (geolocated, June 8); Russia made **limited** confirmed gains near Kupyansk/Lyman; the **Sumy** "buffer zone" effort remains limited and costly; **Kherson** static. **[VERIFIED — ISW/OSINT]**
- **Ukraine has recaptured 600+ km² across 2026** and in late May/early June posted **net territorial gains** (Russia −14 sq mi one week, −38 the prior week). **[VERIFIED — ISW/Russia Matters]**

### Strike war: Ukraine holds the initiative
- **Ukraine's deep-strike campaign has knocked out ~10% of Russian refining** (some estimates up to ~25%, with >30% of gasoline output curtailed); strikes hit ~20 refineries/terminals April–mid-May, reaching ~1,700 km, including the **Moscow-region Kapotnya refinery (June 18)** and a methodical campaign to **sever Crimea** (Kerch, Krasnodar, the North Crimean Canal rail bridge, June 21–23). **[VERIFIED — magnitude DISPUTED]**
- **Russia officially admitted production cuts/shortages for the first time on June 9** — notable after prior denials. **Fuel rationing is in effect** in Moscow/northern Russia (~100–150 L/driver caps), with a **gasoline-export ban** through year-end and below-Euro-5 gasoline permitted to stretch supply. **[VERIFIED]**
- **Russia escalated mass missile/drone barrages** on cities and the energy grid (major June 2 strike on Kyiv; ~105 drones+glide bombs overnight June 21). Ukraine's air defense is strained specifically by **ballistic missiles** (drones mostly intercepted, ballistics less so). The claim that depleted US interceptor stocks from the 2025 Iran war are the cause is **single-source — flagged unconfirmed.** **[VERIFIED barrages / DISPUTED causation]**

### Attrition
- **Russian daily losses ran above 1,000/day for the whole June 1–22 stretch** — the first sustained such run since March 2025 (Ukrainian GS figures, ~1,130–1,550/day). **Treat GS cumulative figures (~1.39M) as one-sided/inflated;** independent estimates are ~1,000,000 Russian casualties (Feb 2026) and ~500,000 Ukrainian "permanent losses" (Dutch MIVD, April 2026). **[DISPUTED — see caveat]**

### Diplomacy: deadline lapsed, no breakthrough
- **The US-pushed June 2026 deadline effectively lapsed** with no ceasefire, no Putin–Zelensky meeting. The Trump framework evolved 28→19 points (Geneva, Nov 2025) — dropping the army cap and amnesty, leaving NATO open — but **Russia holds maximalist demands** (full Ukrainian withdrawal from four oblasts, recognition of annexations, military limits; Lavrov rejected the Ukrainian/European terms on June 7). The main tangible product of talks remains a **~500-each POW exchange** (early March). ISW reads continued strikes + maximalism as the Kremlin **preferring a frozen conflict** over genuine compromise. **[VERIFIED]**

### State of play, one paragraph
Ukraine holds the initiative in the strike/attrition war and has blunted Russia's ground offensive; Russia retains slow grinding pressure at Kostiantynivka/Kupyansk and continues escalating missile-and-drone strikes on the grid. **Neither side has the capacity for a decisive breakthrough as of late June 2026**, and a frozen conflict looks more likely than a settlement.

## Part B — Will Russia Mobilize?

### The trend that changed in 2026
- **Q1 2026 contract recruitment fell ~20% YoY**, to a **three-year low** (~71,000 new contractors vs ~89,600 in Q1 2025), running at roughly ~800/day. **[VERIFIED — Kluge/SWP from Russian budget data; ISW; CNN]**
- **This is now at or below the replacement rate** (~70–80k recruited vs ~85k Ukrainian-estimated casualties / ~30–35k/month per Western officials). The paid-volunteer model is, at the margin, **no longer self-sustaining.** **[ANALYTIC/partly DISPUTED — casualty figures vary]**
- **And it fell *despite* record bonuses** — average sign-on ~1.47M rubles (~$19,300), federal portion doubled. Higher price + lower quantity = a **thinning volunteer pool.** Regions fund most bonuses (up to ~4–10% of their budgets) and several are **nearing fiscal limits** — the binding constraint analysts watch. **[VERIFIED — Kluge]**

### The apparatus built to avoid declaring mobilization
- **Year-round conscription** took effect Jan 1, 2026 (draft offices operate any day; deployments still in two windows). **[VERIFIED]**
- **Digital draft registry + electronic summonses** — an online summons is deemed served after 7 days, auto-triggering travel bans; rolled out region-by-region. **[VERIFIED]**
- **Upper conscription age raised 27→30** (effective 2024), enlarging the pool. **[VERIFIED]**
- **Annual reservist call-up decree** for 2026 "training" (with classified numbers); a **BARS reservist law** allowing up to ~6 months/year of "special musters" to guard infrastructure **without a formal mobilization declaration.** **[VERIFIED — "won't fight in Ukraine" caveat treated skeptically]**
- **Tightened FSB/border exit controls** on draft-age men — a precondition for any large call-up to "stick" without a 2022-style exodus. **[VERIFIED]**

### Why Russia avoids a declared mobilization
- **The 2022 trauma:** the ~300,000 "partial mobilization" triggered protests and an exodus of **~300k–700k** (high end disputed) and a ~10% MOEX drop. **[VERIFIED event / DISPUTED magnitude]**
- **Economic cost:** record-low unemployment (~2–2.5%) amid an **acute labor shortage** — a mass call-up pulls workers from an already-strained economy and competes with defense plants for the same skilled men. CSIS: declaring mobilization would signal the regime is **"politically trapped"** — "a huge gamble." **[VERIFIED/ANALYTIC]**

### The "crypto-mobilization" thesis (the better-supported reading)
- **ISW's central assessment:** Russia is "very likely" drawing on its strategic reserve on a **rolling basis** while deliberately avoiding a single declared mobilization — a **managed covert mobilization.** **[ANALYTIC — ISW, widely echoed]**
- **Concrete mechanics:** regional governors order **enterprises to "select" employees** to sign MoD contracts (firms of 150–500 must produce 2–5 recruits); **street roundups** (Penza, June 2026); ~90B rubles (~$948M) earmarked for contract payments 2025–27. The Kremlin **offloads coercion onto businesses/regions** to deflect blame. **[VERIFIED incidents / ANALYTIC intent]**

### The contrarian signal (flagged weak)
- **Single-source/DISPUTED:** Duma deputy Andrei Gurulev claimed (June 1, 2026, Telegram) a "fundamental decision" for a new large mobilization in **autumn 2026** has "already been made." This is one pro-war legislator citing unnamed sources, **contradicted by official denials and not corroborated by ISW/Kluge/Carnegie.** Raise attention, not confirmation. **[DISPUTED]**

### Assessment: likely or not?
**A declared second general mobilization is not the base case for 2026–27 — but probability is low-to-moderate and rising,** materially higher than a year ago because volunteer recruitment is now slipping below replacement. The base case is **continued and escalating *covert* mobilization** with official denials intact.

**Triggers that would force a declared mobilization (not yet met):**
1. **Imminent/actual front-line collapse** or loss of a major sector (the near-universal threshold analysts cite).
2. **Recruitment falling decisively & durably below losses** *and* **regional budgets unable to fund bonuses** — the volunteer model *breaking*, not just bending. Mid-2026 shows the first cracks, not breakage.
3. **A major escalation** (new front, NATO confrontation, a forced decisive offensive) needing a manpower step-change.
4. **Post-election timing:** if it happens, after the 2026 Duma elections is the logical window (avoid mobilizing before a vote).

## Part C — Would Mobilization Change the Tide?

### The 2022 benchmark
The Sept 2022 partial mobilization (~300,000) **stabilized the front and helped blunt Ukraine's 2023 counteroffensive — but never restored breakthrough capability.** Kofman: "quantity does matter," but mobilization "may not fix the qualitative or force-employment issues." It was a **stabilizing reinforcement, not a tide-turner** — and the conditions that limited it then are *worse* now, while the one advantage it had (relatively intact equipment stocks) has since been depleted. **[ANALYTIC — strong consensus]**

### Three compounding bottlenecks that cap mass-into-combat-power
1. **Training/cadre:** training capacity is saturated and "most competent soldiers have already died"; Russia has **no real NCO pathway**, limiting the small-unit autonomy a mobilized mass needs. Recruit quality is already falling (~40% from "vulnerable groups" — prisoners, debtors). **[FACT/JUDGMENT — RUSI]**
2. **Equipment:** the **Soviet-era tank reserve is nearly spent** (June 2026 satellite analysis: ~2,088 tanks in storage, only ~851 realistically usable); IFV/APC/reactive-artillery stocks at ~16–39% of pre-war; new tank output (~200–250/yr) is **less than losses in a single sector like Pokrovsk.** A new mobilized mass would be **largely dismounted light infantry, not mechanized.** (Minority OSINT view: reserves "shrinking but far from exhausted" — **DISPUTED.**) **[FACT/DISPUTED]**
3. **The drone-saturated battlefield penalizes mass:** drones cause up to ~80% of casualties; **massing forces "results in losses before contact."** Russia already lost ~120 personnel/km² of captured ground in 2025, rising toward ~316/km² in 2026, advancing 15–70 m/day. More men in an attritional meat-grinder mostly **buys more casualties**, not faster advance — and even drone-led tactics "do not allow decisive maneuver to seize terrain." **[FACT/JUDGMENT — consensus]**

### The economic counter-cost
Mobilizing **pulls from the same shrinking labor pool** the war economy depends on (labor reserve down ~40% / ~2.6M workers since 2021; Nabiullina: "never lived in such a shortage of workforce"). IISS frames the core trade-off: Russia "must soon decide whether to **forcibly mobilize its economy and society or scale back war aims.**" Front-line mass and economic sustainability now **compete for the same scarce men.** **[FACT/JUDGMENT]**

### The honest uncertainty (the bull case for impact)
Ukraine's manpower shortage is real and worsening (compounded by reduced US support). **Attrition rewards the side that can keep paying, and Russia's pool is deeper.** If a large mobilization (~400–700k) let Russia sustain pressure across *more* of the line simultaneously while Ukraine thins out, it could **accelerate a slow grind** even without breakthrough — the scenario Ukrainian officials (e.g., MP Kostenko: a 300–500k call-up "will be the turning point") warn about. Counterpoint: Russian casualties have exceeded recruitment every month since late 2025, eroding that edge. **[DISPUTED — the genuine analytic disagreement]**

### Verdict
**A new mobilization would more likely deepen and prolong an attritional grind in Russia's favor at the margins than deliver a clean operational reversal.** Its biggest effects would be felt **slowly — through cumulative attrition — and on Russia's own economy and politics**, not as a sudden shift in the tide. ISW's framing captures it: Russia currently **cannot even form a strategic reserve** (it consumes operational reserves to sustain attacks), so a mobilization would come **"from a place of weakness."**

## Bottom line

- **Now (late June 2026):** stalemate with momentum tilting *away* from Russia — its offensive stalled at the Donbas Fortress Belt, Ukraine holds the strike/attrition initiative (Moscow fuel rationing, Crimea being severed), and diplomacy is frozen after the lapsed June deadline.
- **Mobilization — will they?** A declared general mobilization stays a last resort, but the volunteer model is showing its **first real cracks** (recruitment below replacement despite record pay). Expect **intensified covert mobilization**; a declared one is gated behind a front-collapse-type shock or a post-Duma-election decision.
- **Mobilization — would it matter?** Probably **not decisively.** Russia can't train, equip, or maneuver a fresh mass into breakthrough on a drone-saturated front, and the economic counter-cost is now first-order. The realistic effect is a **longer, deeper attritional grind** — dangerous to a manpower-strained Ukraine over time, but not a clean reversal of the tide, and a move made from weakness rather than strength.
