# Russia–Ukraine War — Part II: Military Capabilities & the Economic Breaking Point (mid-2026)

> **Companion to** `russia-ukraine-war-2026.md`. This part answers two questions in depth: (1) what each side's military can actually do and sustain, and (2) **at what point the financial/economic cost becomes too great for the war to continue** — for Russia, for Ukraine, and for Ukraine's Western backers.
>
> **Method & confidence.** Five parallel research streams, prioritizing IISS, RUSI, CSIS, SIPRI, the Kiel Institute, IMF/World Bank, BOFIT, Carnegie, CEPA, Bruegel, KSE Institute, and OSINT (Oryx, ISW). Claims are tagged **VERIFIED** (official/multi-source data), **ESTIMATE / PROJECTION** (analyst modeling — inherently uncertain), or **DISPUTED** (sources conflict). **Two structural caveats:** (a) all Russian production/loss figures are external estimates (Ukrainian, Baltic, or Western intel) — never Russian-confirmed; (b) automated full-page fetching was blocked (HTTP 403) for many primary outlets, so some exact digits rest on convergent search-summaries of those primaries. Cited URLs should be opened directly before any high-stakes use.

## Headline answer to "when does it become too costly to continue?"

**There is no clean date — but the analysis converges on a clear shape.** Three different clocks are running, and the war ends when one of them runs out *and* the political leadership accepts it:

| Clock | What runs out | Earliest serious bite | Key trigger / swing variable |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Russia — fiscal** | Liquid reserves (NWF ~$48B), oil revenue, future economic capacity | **2027–2028** (late 2026 only if oil stays sub-$40) | **Oil price** (exogenous, volatile) |
| **Ukraine — external financing** | Donor cash for the *civilian* budget (it self-funds defense) | **Already tested (spring 2026)**; recurring annually | **Donor political will** + frozen-asset unlock |
| **The West — political will** | Appetite to keep disbursing (NOT money — the cost is trivial) | **Now / ongoing** | **US absence + EU veto friction** |

**The single most important finding:** for the **West**, support is *economically* affordable almost indefinitely (~0.1–0.7% of GDP) — the constraint is **political will**, not capacity. For **Russia**, the constraint is the reverse — a real but slow *economic* squeeze that most analysts think forces hard trade-offs in 2027–28, not collapse in 2026. For **Ukraine**, the economy is in a stable wartime equilibrium and won't implode on its own; its breaking point is **external** — a donor stoppage. So the war is most likely to be ended by **politics (Western will) or a Russian fiscal threshold around 2027–28**, not by any side's economy physically collapsing in 2026.

## Part A — Military Capabilities In Depth

### A1. The central asymmetry
Russia is **winning the cheap-mass race and losing the high-end-regeneration race**; Ukraine is the mirror image, plus an innovation edge but a manpower deficit. **[ANALYTIC — multi-source consensus]**
- **Russia succeeds at:** artillery shells, Shahed/Geran drones, glide bombs — all scaling *up*.
- **Russia struggles at:** new tanks, microelectronics, naval repair, combat aircraft regeneration — all degrading.
- **Ukraine succeeds at:** FPV/fiber-optic/interceptor drones, domestic artillery (Bohdana), emerging deep-strike missiles.
- **Ukraine struggles at:** manpower, air-defense interceptors, and anything import-dependent (Patriots, ISR, long-range Western fires).

### A2. Russian capabilities (key points)
- **~700,000+ troops committed**, Kremlin targeting ~800,000; but **contract recruitment fell ~20% YoY in Q1 2026** (~800/day), barely replacing losses and not enabling expansion. Sustained only by escalating sign-on bonuses (~1.55M rubles median) — a fiscal stressor. **[ESTIMATE]**
- **Armor is the binding hardware constraint.** IISS assesses Russia may lack enough MBTs for sustained offensive ops *beyond ~2026* at 2024 tempo; it **refurbishes far more than it builds** (~250 new T-90M/yr vs ~1,500 MBTs consumed in 2024), and the best-preserved Soviet storage is largely gone. Russia has adapted with dismounted/motorcycle infantry assaults. **[ESTIMATE — IISS/RUSI]**
- **Artillery: ~7M rounds in 2025** (a ~17× increase since 2021, per Estonian intel), but **barrel production is a bottleneck**, and **North Korean shell supply (~8–12M cumulative) fell sharply in early 2026** as DPRK stocks drew down. **[ESTIMATE]**
- **Air force never achieved air superiority** — kept behind the front by Ukrainian SAMs; relies on **glide bombs (UMPK), a record ~5,700 in Jan 2026**, now extended to ~200 km (jet-powered) with ~400 km in development. **[ESTIMATE]**
- **Missiles:** monthly output ~100–120 Kh-101, ~40–60 Iskander, <10 Kinzhal — a 3–4× wartime increase; stockpiles now trending *up* between salvos. **Oreshnik IRBM** used as strategic signaling. **[ESTIMATE/DISPUTED]**
- **Drones:** Shahed/Geran at **~5,000–5,500/month**, pursuing ~1,000/day; **~95% Chinese-origin components** — Russia's clearest import-substitution success. **[ESTIMATE]**
- **Black Sea Fleet: ~30% destroyed/disabled**; 2026 strikes sank the minesweeper *Valentin Pikul* and disabled the post-*Moskva* flagship *Admiral Makarov*; the fleet faces a maintenance collapse (no dry dock, Bosporus closed). **[VERIFIED losses / ESTIMATE on %]**
- **Nuclear: ~4,400-warhead military stockpile**; revised doctrine lowered the apparent threshold; 2026 exercises assessed as **signaling/intimidation, not use-preparation**. **[VERIFIED/ESTIMATE]**
- **Defense-industrial base:** structural **labor shortage (~160k–250k unfilled skilled jobs)** and continued dependence on Chinese-routed dual-use components (which *fell* in 2025). **[ESTIMATE]**

### A3. Ukrainian capabilities (key points)
- **Force ~677,000 (IISS) to ~800k–1M (other trackers)** — restructured into **18 Army Corps** in 2025. **[DISPUTED size]**
- **Manpower is the acute weakness:** ~200,000 AWOL and ~2M evading draft notices (official claims); **conscription now ~70% of recruitment**; average soldier age **40+**; frontline units often at **~30% strength**. Government **refuses to lower the draft age from 25 to 18** (demographic preservation). Analysts attribute most AWOL to *service conditions* (no rotation/discharge), not collapse of will — Zelensky announced a rotation/discharge reform for mid-2026. **[VERIFIED/ESTIMATE]**
- **Drones are the offset:** Brave1 ecosystem of **2,300+ firms, 1,343 drone models**; targeting **7M+ drones in 2026**; **interceptor drones a breakout** (Zelensky claims ~2,000/day capacity; the Pentagon wants to buy them). **[OFFICIAL CLAIM/VERIFIED interest]**
- **Domestic deep-strike maturing** — three "programs of record": **Flamingo FP-5** (claimed ~3,000 km/1,150 kg warhead, ~3/day), **Long Neptune** (~1,000 km), **Sapsan** ballistic (now in combat). Explicitly built to escape Western range/approval limits. Production scale is single-source and **DISPUTED**; Russia has struck these production lines. **[OFFICIAL CLAIM/DISPUTED]**
- **Domestic artillery: Bohdana 155mm at ~40/month**, ~40% of front-line artillery, ~95% local content. **[VERIFIED/OFFICIAL]**
- **Air defense — the critical vulnerability:** a **severe Patriot/PAC-3 interceptor shortage**, worsened when the 2025 US–Iran war consumed large US Patriot stocks. Overall interception ~90% (Mar 2026), but **ballistic/hypersonic missiles are the weak point (~74%)**, and Russia deliberately **mass-fires ballistics to saturate the scarce PAC-3 layer** (one June barrage: 41 ballistic missiles, ~30 hit). **[VERIFIED]**
- **Deep-strike is strategically effective:** Ukrainian strikes took an estimated **~25–40% of Russian refining offline** (the 40% is Ukrainian-official; independents say ~25% — **DISPUTED**), driving **fuel rationing to Moscow** by June 2026 — effectively "long-range sanctions." **[VERIFIED effect / DISPUTED magnitude]**
- **What breaks if US aid stops:** hardest to replace are **(a) air-defense interceptors, (b) intelligence/targeting, (c) Starlink C2, (d) long-range Western fires.** RUSI notes cutting *targeting/technical assistance* would bite faster than halting the arms pipeline. Increasingly self-sufficient: FPV/interceptor drones, Bohdana, domestic deep-strike. **[ESTIMATE — think-tank consensus]**

### A4. Sustainability bottom line (military)
**Neither side is likely to collapse militarily in 2026.** Russia is a materially declining force grinding forward tactically while its armor/labor ceilings tighten through 2026; Ukraine can sustain the defense on current trajectory but under worsening manpower strain and an air-defense interceptor crisis that depends on Western resupply. **[ESTIMATE — IISS/CSIS/RUSI]**

## Part B — The Economic Breaking Point

### B1. Russia: a slow squeeze, not a 2026 cliff

**The data shows real strain but no crisis dynamics** (no default, no bank run, no hyperinflation):
- **2026 defense budget ~14.9T rubles (~6.3% of GDP, ~38% of federal spending)** — notably, the **first nominal defense-spending cut** of the war. **[VERIFIED — SIPRI]**
- **Deficit running hot:** the **first four months of 2026 already produced a ~5.9T-ruble deficit** — exceeding the *entire* planned 2026 figure and full-year 2025. **[VERIFIED — KSE/Foreign Affairs]**
- **VAT raised 20%→22%** (Jan 2026); **seven consecutive deficit years** planned (unprecedented since 1999). **[VERIFIED]**
- **National Wealth Fund (the key buffer): liquid assets ~$47.8B (≈1.7% of GDP) as of April 2026**, down from ~$185B pre-war, drawn at a record pace. **[VERIFIED]**
- **Oil & gas revenue fell ~50% YoY in early 2026**; the **dynamic price cap dropped to ~$44/bbl**; Urals briefly sold **below $35** in Dec 2025. **[VERIFIED]**
- **THE BIG WILDCARD — the Iran/Hormuz oil shock:** in spring 2026 a conflict involving Iran pushed Urals to a **13-year high (~$112–125/bbl)**, temporarily **reversing the fiscal squeeze** and prompting the IMF to *raise* Russia's 2026 growth forecast. This shows Russia's breaking point is **highly sensitive to an exogenous, volatile oil price.** **[VERIFIED — but inherently temporary]**
- **Key rate was *cut*** from a record 21% to **14.5%** (Apr 2026) — the central bank believes overheating is easing; inflation projected ~4.5–5.5%. **[VERIFIED]**
- **Visible civilian stress:** **fuel rationing in Moscow** and northern Russia (June 2026) from Ukrainian refinery strikes; refinery throughput at a 16-year low. **[VERIFIED]**

**The expert range on Russia's breaking point:**
- **Most bearish (acute 2026–27 crunch):** Gazprombank's own forecasters (NWF dry in ~1.3 years at low oil), RANEPA/Gaidar (NWF could empty in 2026 if oil stays cheap) — **all conditional on oil ~$40 or below**, which the Iran shock temporarily voided.
- **Mainstream (slow erosion, binds hard ~2027–28):** BOFIT, World Bank, IIF, *Foreign Affairs* ("fiscal indicators flashing red" but "**does not face imminent economic collapse**").
- **Most resilient (years more):** Carnegie's Prokopenko ("altitude sickness / negative equilibrium" — can fight "for the foreseeable future" while cannibalizing its future); CEPA ("two of three" — sacrifices growth; explicitly **"no currency run, debt crisis, or bread queues in 2027"**).

**Russia synthesis:** No analyst consensus for a 2026 collapse — that's an outlier view. The genuine constraint is a **slow squeeze contingent on oil price.** The clearest breaking-point trigger in the data is **sustained Urals at $30–40/bbl** (empties the NWF fastest, forces money-printing or hard defense cuts). Absent that, painful war-altering fiscal pressure lands in **2027–2028**, and even then it looks like forced *trade-offs and stagnation* rather than collapse. **Critically (Carnegie):** Putin's motivation is political, so fiscal pain may not translate into stopping.

### B2. Ukraine: no internal collapse point — the breaking point is external

- **Ukraine self-funds its *entire* defense from domestic revenue and borrowing; donors fund the *civilian* budget** (pensions, salaries, healthcare). This is the single most important structural fact. **[VERIFIED — Finance Minister Marchenko]**
- **2026 budget:** ~$113.8B spending; **defense ~$66.4B (~27% of GDP)**; **deficit ~$41.5B (~18.4% of GDP)**. Analysts call it "unrealistic" — it under-budgets defense vs. a true ~$120B need. **[VERIFIED/DISPUTED]**
- **External financing need: ~$52B in 2026 alone**; IMF puts the **2026–27 gap at ~€137B**, with a residual ~$63B unfilled before the EU deal. **[VERIFIED]**
- **THE SPRING 2026 CLIFF (already tested):** Bloomberg/officials reported usable **defense funds running out by ~June 2026** amid the US halt and Hungary's veto. **Averted narrowly** by the **€90B EU loan** (final approval April 23, 2026; disbursement began late May/June). **[VERIFIED]**
- **The €90B EU loan is funded by EU capital-markets borrowing, NOT frozen assets** — covering ~two-thirds of the need; a remaining ~€30B was pushed onto non-EU partners (Canada, Japan, UK, Norway). **[VERIFIED]**
- **Macro is stable:** reserves ~$45.7B (~4.7 months imports), inflation ~9.4%, hryvnia managed, growth ~1.3–2%. **But public debt rises to ~123% of GDP (2026), peaking ~137% (2027).** **[VERIFIED]**
- **The binding-constraint debate:** **Carnegie (Mar 2026) says it's now MONEY, not raw manpower** — there are still millions of eligible men but no funds to reform mobilization, and an aid shortfall would force **cuts to drone production** (Ukraine's key offset). Others say manpower is the acute "inflection." **[DISPUTED]**

**Ukraine synthesis:** **There is no purely economic breaking point where Ukraine's economy implodes in 2026.** The breaking point is **political-external — a donor funding stoppage.** Minimum sustaining aid is roughly **$45–52B/yr budget financing + ~$15–25B weapons ≈ $60–70B/yr.** That floor is being met for 2026, but it's **assembled at the last minute every year**, contingent on EU unanimity, the frozen-asset debate, and continued US absence. The decisive swing factor is the **frozen ~$300B in Russian assets**: unlocking a reparations-loan mechanism would de-risk 2026–28; keeping it blocked while political will erodes flips the constraint back to money.

### B3. The West: affordable almost indefinitely — the limit is political will

- **The cost is trivial relative to capacity:** Bruegel — replacing the US would cost the EU only **~0.12% of GDP**; Ukraine's *total* package is **~0.6–0.7% of EU GDP/year**. CEPA — total Western support is **<0.2% of combined Western GDP** annualized and only **~4% of Western defense budgets**. **There is no economic ceiling forcing the West to quit.** **[ANALYTIC — Bruegel/CEPA]**
- **Europe has overtaken the US** (>$235B vs ~$135B cumulative) but **is NOT fully replacing lost US support** — Kiel's "Europe fails to offset US aid drop"; **US military aid fell ~99% in 2025.** **[VERIFIED — Kiel]**
- **The US shifted to "buy-don't-give" (PURL):** allies pay for US weapons (~$1B/month; >$4B pledged by Dec 2025; 24 countries). The US still provides **defensive intelligence and Starlink.** **[VERIFIED]**
- **Political friction is the real constraint:** Hungary/Slovakia repeatedly **vetoed** the €90B loan (lifted only after the Druzhba pipeline was repaired — an explicit transactional linkage); **Belgium and the ECB killed the "reparations loan"** as legally risky, forcing the EU onto its own balance sheet. The freeze was made quasi-permanent; **Russia's central bank is suing the EU.** **[VERIFIED]**
- **The genuinely expensive problem is broader European self-defense** without the US (~€250B/yr toward 3.5% of GDP) — **not Ukraine aid itself.** **[ANALYTIC/DISPUTED]**

**West synthesis:** Western support is **economically affordable but politically constrained.** The scarce resource is the **velocity and reliability of disbursement**, not the size of the wallet. A US that has already left grant-funding, plus enough European veto/fatigue friction to make disbursement intermittent, **can starve Ukraine toward a settlement well before Europe hits any real fiscal limit.**

## Part C — How Economics Actually Ends This War: Three Scenarios

From CSIS, Carnegie, ECFR, Bruegel scenario modeling. CSIS rates a **prolonged stalemate the most probable 2026–28 outcome**; ~60% of wars historically end in compromise, but only once both sides converge on a shared read of relative power — which **has not yet happened.**

1. **Attritional exhaustion (Russia cracks first).** *Trigger:* NWF depletion (2027–28) + sub-$40 oil + record deficits force Moscow to choose between war and welfare. *Status:* fiscal stress is **VERIFIED**, but "collapse" is **DISPUTED** — Carnegie warns Putin's driver is political, so fiscal pain may not force termination. This is the path where **Russia's economic clock is the harder *literal* constraint.**

2. **Frozen conflict / prolonged stalemate (the modal forecast).** *Trigger:* Western aid settles into a "just-enough" equilibrium (€90B loan + PURL + SAFE) that prevents Ukrainian collapse but can't enable advance, while Russia degrades slowly. *Economic tipping point:* **sufficiency without victory** on both sides.

3. **Negotiated settlement under donor-fatigue pressure (the Trump-leverage path).** *Trigger:* US drawdown + Europe's inability to fully backfill + the dead reparations-loan shortens Ukraine's financing runway, pushing Kyiv toward territorial/NATO compromise. ECFR/Carnegie see scaled-back support as **the economic lever that converts the war into a settlement** — with a sharp dispute over whether that settlement is durable peace or effective **capitulation**. Here the frozen $300B becomes a **negotiating chip**, not a war-financing engine.

## Bottom line

- **"When does it become too costly to continue?"** — There is **no single economic breaking point in 2026.** The most-cited analytic claim is that **Russia is more likely to be ended by economics (2027–28, oil-price-contingent) than the West is** — but **Western *political will* is the more fragile variable**, and it can force a Ukrainian settlement *before* anyone hits a literal economic wall.
- **Russia** can pay for the war through 2026; genuine war-altering fiscal pain is a **2027–2028** story unless oil stays below ~$40. The constraint is a slow squeeze on its *future*, not an imminent crash.
- **Ukraine** has no internal economic collapse point; it can fight as long as **donors deliver ~$60–70B/yr** — a floor met for 2026 but renegotiated, narrowly, every year. The **frozen $300B** is the decisive lever.
- **The West** can afford this almost indefinitely (~0.1–0.7% of GDP). The limit is **political will and disbursement reliability**, not money.
- **Most probable near-term path (CSIS):** prolonged attritional **stalemate**, with the economics setting the terms of an eventual negotiated settlement rather than producing a sudden collapse on either side.
