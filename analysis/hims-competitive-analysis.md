## Slide 1 — Scope: HIMS at the center, four competitive postures arrayed around it

- **Why this framing.** HIMS sits at a strange intersection. Telehealth peers (TDOC, AMWL) compete on visit infrastructure but have a different business model (B2B + provider-led). DTC point-solution peers (Ro, Noom) compete head-to-head on consumer acquisition. GLP-1 incumbents (LLY, NVO) are simultaneously the supplier of branded drugs *and* the direct-channel competitor pressuring compounded GLP-1 economics. Vertical-integration giants (AMZN, CVS, WMT) are the long-term existential threat to consumer-pay healthcare.
- **What's in / out.** Excluded: BetterHelp (mental-health-only, part of TDOC), Talkspace (mental health only, small), traditional pharmacy benefit managers. Included as "adjacent threats" only — AMZN/CVS/WMT — because they don't operate on the same DTC subscription model but could.
- **Period.** HIMS FY24 (Dec-end) anchored, with FY25 outlook where guided.

## Slide 3 — Industry economics: DTC subscription unit economics

**Where the dollars come from.**
- **Subscription revenue.** ~95% of HIMS revenue is recurring subscription [E]. Customer pays cash; no insurance reimbursement loop.
- **AOV expansion.** Multi-category cross-sell (start in hair, add MH, add weight loss) lifts ARPU from ~$50/mo to ~$150/mo for personalized stacks [E].
- **Unit economics structure.** Acquisition cost ~$80-120 [E] per subscriber; payback period 6-9 months for core categories; gross margin ~75%+ on personalized/compounded SKUs vs. ~70% on standard SKUs.

**Where competitors capture differently.**
- *Legacy telehealth (TDOC, AMWL)* — provider visit fees + B2B contracts with payers/employers. Lower gross margin (~55-65%), no consumer brand pull-through.
- *DTC point-solution (Ro, Noom)* — same model as HIMS but typically single-category or two-category.
- *GLP-1 incumbents (LLY, NVO)* — branded drug margin (~80%+ at list price), but battling for cash-pay channel via direct programs at lower prices ($349-499/mo Zepbound, $199 Wegovy at cash).
- *Vertical integration (AMZN, CVS)* — fulfillment scale + existing customer relationships; subscription not the central model.

**Takeaway insight (slide title).** "HIMS's edge is multi-category subscription stacking — competitors are either narrower (Ro, Noom) or operate on different economic models (TDOC, LLY)."

## Slide 8 — GLP-1 incumbents — the bull/bear pivot point

### The compounding regulatory dynamic

- **FDA shortage list mechanics.** Compounding pharmacies can produce non-branded versions of drugs on the FDA shortage list. Semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic) and tirzepatide (Zepbound/Mounjaro) were both on the shortage list through most of 2024.
- **2024-2025 transition.** FDA removed tirzepatide from shortage list in late 2024; semaglutide removal followed. This *prima facie* ends mass compounded GLP-1.
- **The personalization carve-out.** Compounding remains legal for "personalized" formulations (different dose, added vitamin, combination product) under 503A. HIMS is positioning as a personalization specialist post-shortage.
- **Legal pressure.** LLY and NVO have filed multiple lawsuits against compounding pharmacies and telehealth distributors. Outcomes pending.

| Lemonaid | Medium | Low | Mid-low |

**Build note.** HIMS alone in upper-right. Ro is the closest neighbor. LLY/NVO are bottom-left — they have depth but no breadth.

### 9b — Moat scorecard

| Moat | HIMS | What's underneath |
|---|---|---|
| **Network effects** | Weak — DTC consumer business has limited network effects | Personalization data improves with scale, but not classic network effect |
| **Switching costs** | Moderate | Subscription routine + auto-shipped Rx + personalized formulations create friction, not technical lock-in |
| **Scale economies** | Moderate | CAC efficiency at scale (paid media); manufacturing scale on personalized compounded SKUs |
| **Intangibles** | Strong | Brand (the rare DTC health brand consumers actually know); regulatory/clinical infrastructure; personalization IP |

**Durable advantages.**
- Multi-category subscription bundling (no other DTC peer has the same breadth at scale)
- Brand — Super Bowl ads, athlete partnerships, broad consumer awareness
- Personalization at scale — compounded SKUs across categories

**Structural vulnerabilities.**
- GLP-1 regulatory dependency — compounded GLP-1 was a major growth driver and is regulatory-fragile
- Cash-pay dependency — no payer/employer revenue diversification
- Competitive crowding — barriers to entry in DTC telehealth are low

**Takeaway insight (slide title).** "HIMS's moat is brand + breadth, not network or technical lock-in — durable in core categories, vulnerable in GLP-1."
